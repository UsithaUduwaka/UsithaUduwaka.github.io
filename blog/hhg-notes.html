Notes on High Harmonic Generation

Physics Notes · June 2023 · 12 min read

Personal notes from working through the theory of HHG while building the simulation. Covers the three-step model, the TDSE setup, and some intuition I found helpful that most textbooks skip.

Why HHG is surprising

The first time I saw an HHG spectrum I found it genuinely strange: you drive a single atom with 800 nm light and it emits 25th, 27th, 29th harmonics — coherent XUV radiation from a single electron. Classical electrodynamics says a free electron in a laser field emits only at the driving frequency (Thomson scattering). So something fundamentally quantum must be happening.

The key is the interplay between the bound state, the continuum, and the laser. The electron doesn't just oscillate — it actually leaves the atom, propagates classically for half a cycle, and then comes back. The recollision energy converts to photon energy. That's the semiclassical picture, and it's remarkably accurate.

The three-step model (quick derivation)

Assume the electron starts at rest at $x = 0$ at time $t_0$ (birth time). In the laser field $E(t) = E_0 \cos(\omega t)$, Newton's law gives:

$$\ddot{x} = -E_0\cos(\omega t)$$

Integrating twice with $\dot{x}(t_0) = 0$:

$$\dot{x}(t) = -\frac{E_0}{\omega}\bigl[\sin(\omega t) - \sin(\omega t_0)\bigr]$$
$$x(t) = \frac{E_0}{\omega^2}\bigl[\cos(\omega t) - \cos(\omega t_0)\bigr] + \frac{E_0}{\omega}\sin(\omega t_0)\,(t - t_0)$$

The return time $t_r$ is found by solving $x(t_r) = 0$. At return, the kinetic energy is:

$$E_k(t_r) = \frac{1}{2}\dot{x}(t_r)^2$$

Maximizing over all birth times $t_0$, the maximum kinetic energy at return is $3.17\,U_p$, so the emitted photon energy is at most:

$$\hbar\omega_{\max} = I_p + 3.17\,U_p$$

This is the cutoff law — one of those rare analytic results in strong-field physics that just works.

Intuition check The $3.17\,U_p$ factor comes purely from classical mechanics — no quantum input. The quantum part (tunneling, recombination matrix element) determines the harmonic yield and phase, but not the cutoff position. I found this separation of classical and quantum surprisingly clean.

Setting up the TDSE numerically

For the 1D simulation I needed three things: a grid, a potential, and a propagator.

Grid: $N = 1024$ points, $x \in [-100, 100]$ a.u. That's large enough that the wavepacket doesn't hit the edges mid-simulation. Atomic units simplify everything — $\hbar = m_e = e = a_0 = 1$.

Potential: The real hydrogen potential $V = -1/|x|$ is singular at $x=0$. The standard fix is the soft-core:

$$V(x) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}}$$

With $a = 1$, the ground state energy comes out to $E_0 \approx -0.67$ a.u., which gives $I_p \approx 0.67$ a.u. — close enough to hydrogen for our purposes.

Propagator: At each timestep I need to apply $e^{-i\hat{H}\Delta t}$ to the wavefunction without forming the full matrix exponential (which is $N \times N$ and expensive). The trick is the Krylov-subspace method:

$$e^{-i\hat{H}\Delta t}|\psi\rangle \approx \sum_{k=0}^{m} c_k\,\hat{H}^k|\psi\rangle$$

where $m \ll N$ (I used $m = 20$). This is essentially what scipy.sparse.linalg.expm_multiply does under the hood.

# The one-liner that does all the heavy lifting
from scipy.sparse.linalg import expm_multiply

psi_next = expm_multiply(-1j * H_sparse * dt, psi)

I initially tried a split-operator method (alternating $e^{-i T \Delta t/2}$ and $e^{-i V \Delta t}$ in Fourier space) but the time-dependent laser term couples position and momentum, which makes the factorization approximate. The Krylov method handles the full Hamiltonian directly.

Absorbing boundaries

Without absorbing boundaries the ionized wavepacket hits the grid edges and reflects back — completely unphysical. The fix is a complex absorbing potential (CAP) added at the edges:

$$W(x) = -i\,\eta \cdot \max(|x| - x_0, 0)^2$$

where $x_0 = 80$ a.u. and $\eta = 10^{-3}$ a.u. The imaginary part causes probability density to decay rather than reflect. Getting $\eta$ right matters: too large and you absorb the physical wavepacket, too small and the CAP doesn't work fast enough.

Extracting the spectrum

The HHG spectrum comes from the Fourier transform of the dipole acceleration, not the dipole moment. The acceleration form suppresses the low-frequency contribution from the bound state and gives a cleaner plateau:

$$a(t) = \left\langle\psi(t)\left|-\frac{dV}{dx} - E(t)\right|\psi(t)\right\rangle$$

I apply a Hann window before the FFT to reduce spectral leakage from the finite pulse duration. The resulting spectrum shows odd harmonics only (even harmonics are suppressed by inversion symmetry of the potential and the monochromatic field).

Gotcha I initially forgot to subtract the mean of $a(t)$ before the FFT. This leaves a DC component that shows up as a spurious peak at $\omega = 0$ and bleeds into the low-harmonic region via spectral leakage. Always detrend the signal first.

What I'd do differently

  1. Use a Gaussian basis instead of a real-space grid — much fewer basis functions needed for the same accuracy.
  2. Implement the iSURF boundary condition instead of CAP for more accurate ionization yields.
  3. Track individual quantum trajectories (short vs. long) to understand attosecond pulse generation — the full quantum picture is richer than the simple spectrum.
